Before You Start

The Mindset You Need

Violin is famously the hardest instrument to start — but also one of the most rewarding. Read this section first. It will save you from quitting in Week 3.

The Honest Truth: For the first 2–4 weeks, your violin will sound like a cat screaming. Every beginner goes through this. It is not a sign that you have no talent — it is the normal physics of learning bow control. Push through this wall and the reward on the other side is extraordinary.

What to Expect

  • Weeks 1–4: Painful screeching sounds. Normal.
  • Weeks 4–8: First clean, warm tones emerge
  • Months 2–4: Scales sound musical
  • Month 5–7: Simple melodies playable
  • Month 8–12: Playing recognizable songs
  • Month 12+: Playing songs by ear comfortably

The Self-Taught Rules

  • Record yourself every week. You can't hear your own bad habits in real-time
  • 30 min/day beats 3 hours on Sunday. Daily contact is everything
  • Slow is fast. Never rush a technique
  • Posture is the foundation — fix it before learning anything else
  • If it hurts, you are doing something wrong. Stop and fix.
Indian Music Advantage: Your familiarity with Indian music — hearing ragas, bhajans, film songs — is a massive asset. Your ears are already trained on microtones, ornaments (gamakas), and complex melodic patterns that Western students never encounter. This will accelerate your ear training dramatically in Phase 5.

Step Zero

Equipment — What to Buy

Do not spend money on a cheap toy violin. A bad instrument will make learning 10× harder and actively teach you wrong habits. Here is every item you need.

Avoid: Any violin sold for under ₹2000 on Amazon/Flipkart. These are toys — bad tone, bad intonation, bad bow response. They will frustrate you into quitting. The budget below is the absolute minimum for a functional learning instrument.
🎻
Violin (4/4 Full Size)
₹5,000 – ₹12,000

Buy a 4/4 size (full size for adults). Look for brands like Kadence, Stentor Student I, or buy from a local music shop where you can inspect it. Ask for a violin with ebony fittings (chin rest, pegs, fingerboard). Avoid decorated "gift" violins.

🏹
Bow (Brazilwood or Fiberglass)
Often Included / ₹800–₹2,000

Most student violins come with a bow. If buying separately, get a fiberglass bow — more durable for beginners, less sensitive to humidity. Make sure the bow hair is white (not yellow/brown). Check that it can be tightened and loosened.

🧱
Rosin (Dark Rosin)
₹100 – ₹400

Absolutely essential. Without rosin, the bow makes no sound. Buy dark rosin (like Pirastro Goldflex or Hill Dark) — it grips better in Indian humidity. Apply to new bow: 40–50 slow strokes. Daily use: 5–8 strokes.

💆
Shoulder Rest
₹300 – ₹800

Non-negotiable for self-taught. Without it, you will hunch, tense your shoulder, and develop bad posture. Get a Kun Collapsible or similar. It clips onto the bottom of the violin. Saves your neck and shoulder from injury.

🎵
Chromatic Tuner App
Free

Download GuitarTuna or insTuner on your phone. Tune your violin before every single practice session. Eventually you will tune by ear, but for now use the app always. Set it to violin (G D A E strings).

📐
Music Stand
₹400 – ₹900

You need your sheet music / notes at eye level so you don't bend your neck down while playing. A basic folding music stand is enough. Alternatively, a raised phone/tablet stand works for watching tutorial videos.

🧻
Soft Cloth / Microfiber
₹50 – ₹150

Wipe rosin dust off the body of the violin after every session. Rosin accumulates and hardens on the wood, dulling the varnish over time. Never use paper towels — they scratch the finish.

📦
Case / Hard Case
Usually Included

Most student violins come with a case. Always store your violin in the case. Keep the case away from direct sunlight, AC vents, and fans. Indian humidity changes can crack the wood. Store extra rosin and a spare string (E string) in the case pocket.

🔕
Practice Mute (Optional)
₹100 – ₹300

A rubber mute clips onto the bridge and reduces volume by ~70%. Useful if practicing late at night when you don't want to disturb family. Don't use it too often — it changes the feel of the bow. For daytime, always play without mute.

Strings

About Strings

Most student violins come with synthetic strings. This is fine. Standard tuning: G D A E (lowest to highest). Replace strings if they are rusty, unraveling, or won't stay in tune. A set of D'Addario Prelude strings (₹600–₹900) is the best beginner option. Keep a spare E string — it snaps most often.

Day 1 Tasks

Setup, Tuning & Care

Before you play a single note, you need to know your instrument — its parts, how to tune it, and how to care for it daily.

Parts of the Violin You Must Know

The Body

  • Scroll — Decorative top of the instrument
  • Pegbox & Pegs — For tuning (coarse). Turn slowly.
  • Neck & Fingerboard — Where your left hand goes
  • Body / Top Plate — The resonating wood top
  • f-holes — Two slots in the top for sound projection
  • Bridge — The wooden piece strings pass over. Keeps strings up off the fingerboard. Extremely important — keep it straight.
  • Tailpiece — Holds the strings at the bottom
  • Fine Tuners — Metal screws in the tailpiece for micro-tuning
  • Chin Rest — Mounts on the bottom left

The Bow

  • Frog — The end you hold. Has a screw.
  • Stick — The wooden shaft
  • Hair — White horsehair stretched between frog and tip
  • Tip — The far end of the bow
  • Screw / Adjuster — Turn clockwise to tighten hair before playing. Counter-clockwise to loosen after playing.

Bow Hair Tension: Tighten until hair is ~1cm from stick at the widest point. Never over-tighten — you'll warp the bow stick. Always loosen after playing.

How to Tune (Do This Every Session)

1

Open your tuner app and set it to chromatic or violin mode

The four open strings of the violin are, from lowest to highest: G — D — A — E. Pluck each string and watch the tuner.

2

Use fine tuners first (the screws on the tailpiece)

Fine tuners adjust pitch slightly. Turn clockwise to raise pitch, counter-clockwise to lower. Use these for most tuning. Only use the pegs when the string is very far out of tune.

3

Using the pegs — do this slowly and carefully

Pegs are friction-fit and can snap strings if turned too fast. Hold the violin steady, turn the peg slowly, and push it inward slightly as you turn to seat it firmly so it doesn't slip back.

4

Always tune up to the note, not down to it

Approach the correct pitch from below (slightly flat) and tune up. Tuning down to a note causes string slippage. Always overshoot slightly low, then tune up.

5

Check all four strings before every session

Indian climate (heat, humidity) causes significant tuning drift. Your violin will go out of tune between sessions. This is normal. Tune before every session, even if you tuned the day before.

Daily Care Routine

✓ Always Do

  • Wipe rosin dust off strings and top of violin with cloth after every session
  • Loosen bow hair after every session
  • Store violin in case away from windows and fans
  • Check bridge is standing straight (not leaning)
  • Keep violin away from AC drafts
  • Wash hands before playing

✗ Never Do

  • Leave violin out of case overnight
  • Place violin on its back/front on a hard surface — always in case or on stand
  • Touch the bow hair with your fingers (oils ruin the rosin grip)
  • Leave bow at full tension after playing
  • Place violin near direct sunlight or a heater
  • Drop or knock the bridge — it is only held by string tension
Bridge Warning: If your bridge falls over, don't panic. It is held up only by string tension. Carefully stand it back up with the flat side facing the tailpiece and the curved side facing the fingerboard. The feet must sit flat on the body. If you're unsure, take it to a music shop.

Weeks 1–6

Phase 1: First Sounds

The entire goal of Phase 1 is posture and producing a clean open string tone. Do not try to play songs. Do not skip ahead. Everything in your future playing depends on what you build here.

Phase 1 Goal: By the end of 6 weeks, you should be able to draw the bow across all four open strings and produce a clean, warm (not scratchy) tone. That's it. One skill. But it will take 6 weeks of daily work to truly nail it.
01.1

How to Hold the Violin

Master before touching the bow — Week 1

▼

Standing Posture

  • Stand straight, feet shoulder-width apart, slight bend in knees
  • Relax your shoulders — tension is your biggest enemy
  • Weight evenly on both feet. Do not lean.
  • You can also sit on a chair — sit on the edge, not leaning back

Placing the Violin

  • Rest the violin on your left collarbone (not on the shoulder)
  • The violin rests between your collarbone and your jaw/chin
  • The chin rest goes under your jaw — you grip it with your jaw, not with your hand
  • Use the shoulder rest to fill the gap between violin and shoulder
  • The violin points roughly towards 1–2 o'clock (left diagonal)
  • Scroll (tip of violin) should be at or above eye level — not drooping down
  • The violin should stay in place without your left hand touching it. Test this: hold violin with chin/jaw only, release left hand. If violin falls, your hold is wrong.

Left Hand and Arm Position

  • Left elbow comes under the violin, pointing toward the right (inward)
  • Left wrist is straight — not bent in or out. This is critical.
  • Thumb rests lightly on the left side of the neck — not under the neck, not gripping
  • Thumb tip touches the neck gently — it should NOT be stiff or white from pressure
  • Fingers are curved, hovering over the strings like they are resting on a small ball
  • Never collapse the wrist or pull the scroll toward you with your hand
Neck Squeeze Alert: The most common beginner error is squeezing the neck between thumb and index finger. This locks up your hand and causes immense tension. The violin is supported by your chin/jaw and shoulder rest. Your left hand is there only to guide fingers — not to hold the violin up.
01.2

The Bow Hold — Most Critical Skill

Spend the most time here — Week 1–2

▼
This is the hardest thing to learn from a video/text. Watch multiple YouTube videos on bow hold. Look up "Russian bow hold" and "Franco-Belgian bow hold" — for Indian music, either works, but Franco-Belgian is more common. The steps below describe the Franco-Belgian hold.

Step-by-Step Bow Hold

  • Step 1: Hold the bow stick in your right hand, pointing away from you. Bow hair faces down.
  • Step 2: Curve your right thumb and place it on the underside of the stick, at the frog. Thumb tip touches the stick. Thumb is curved outward like a frog's leg — never stiff or locked.
  • Step 3: Place the middle finger directly opposite the thumb on top of the stick
  • Step 4: Ring finger rests next to middle finger on top of stick
  • Step 5: Index finger drapes over the stick at an angle — the stick rests between the first and second knuckle of the index finger. Index finger controls bow pressure.
  • Step 6: Pinky finger curves and rests lightly on top of the stick. It is a balance point, not a gripper.
  • All fingers are curved. No finger is straight or stiff.
  • The grip should feel like you are holding a small bird — firm enough that it won't fly away, gentle enough that you won't crush it.

Bow Hold Exercises (Do Daily)

  • Bow Hold Without Violin: Just practice the bow hold in the air. Check in a mirror. Do it 5 minutes daily until it's automatic.
  • Pencil Exercise: Practice the bow hold with a pencil first — it's lighter and easier. Use for 5 minutes before switching to the bow.
  • Spider Exercise: Hold the bow vertically, frog up. Walk your fingers up and down the stick using only finger movement. Trains finger flexibility.
  • Bow Balance: Hold the bow at the balance point (~1/3 from frog) horizontally. Practice keeping it level without tension.
01.3

Applying Rosin Correctly

Before your first bowing attempt

▼

How to Apply Rosin

  • Tighten the bow hair first (about 1cm gap from stick)
  • Scratch the surface of the rosin block with a key or coin (new rosin needs this to create powder)
  • Hold the rosin block in one hand, bow in other
  • Draw the bow hair from frog to tip across the rosin, slowly and with light pressure
  • Repeat 5–8 times for regular use. New bow: 40–50 times.
  • Never touch the bow hair with your fingers — skin oils prevent rosin adhesion
  • Too much rosin = harsh, scratchy tone and white powder clouds. Too little = no sound, bow slides.
01.4

First Bowing — Open Strings

Weeks 1–4: This is your entire practice

▼

Bow Path — The Contact Point

  • Draw the bow between the bridge and the fingerboard — in the middle of this gap is the contact point (sounding point)
  • Too close to bridge → harsh, glassy, thin tone
  • Too far from bridge (over fingerboard) → weak, airy, fuzzy tone
  • The sweet spot is ~2–3cm from the bridge. This is where you aim every stroke.
  • Bow must stay at a 90-degree angle to the string — parallel to the bridge. Watch your bow path in a mirror. It must not drift toward the fingerboard or bridge.

The Bow Arm Motion

  • Down bow (pushing away → tip): Your elbow leads the motion. Wrist adjusts slightly at the end of the stroke to keep the bow straight.
  • Up bow (pulling toward → frog): Your arm pulls back. The elbow rises naturally near the frog.
  • The motion is horizontal. Your elbow adjusts height depending on which string you're on.
  • For the E string: elbow is lower
  • For the G string: elbow is higher (almost pointing to the ceiling)
  • Never rotate your wrist to bow — rotate at the shoulder/elbow, not the wrist.

Week 1–2 Practice: Only Open Strings

  • Bow each open string (G, D, A, E) separately using the middle of the bow
  • Do 10 full down-bows on each string, then 10 up-bows
  • Listen for: is it warm? Is it clear? No scratch? No wobble?
  • Slow bow speed = fuller, warmer tone. Fast bow speed = bright, thin tone.
  • Practice long, slow, even bow strokes first — from frog to tip in 5 seconds
  • Don't press hard. Let the weight of the bow arm do the work.

Week 3–4: String Crossings

  • Practice moving from one string to the next smoothly
  • G→D→A→E and back. Each gets 4 slow bows.
  • String changes happen from the elbow — the whole arm drops or rises slightly
  • Don't tilt the bow — raise/lower the arm to change strings
  • Practice slowly. String crossing noise (accidental double-stops) should disappear.

Week 5–6: Bow Speed and Pressure Experiments

  • Experiment consciously: try fast bow + light pressure, slow bow + more pressure
  • Find the combination that produces your clearest, warmest tone
  • Try different contact points (closer and farther from bridge) to hear the difference
  • This experimentation trains your ear-hand coordination, the core skill for playing by ear later

Weeks 7–16

Phase 2: Left Hand & First Notes

Now the left hand joins the right. This is where real music starts to emerge. You will learn finger placement, scales, and play your first simple melodies.

Phase 2 Goal: Play a clean 2-octave D major scale. Play "Twinkle Twinkle" or a simple bhajan melody slowly. Your intonation (tuning of individual notes) will still be imperfect — that is fine at this stage.
02.1

Finger Numbers & Finger Placement

The language of the left hand — Week 7

▼

Finger Numbering System

  • 0 = Open string (no finger down)
  • 1 = Index finger
  • 2 = Middle finger
  • 3 = Ring finger
  • 4 = Little finger (pinky)

How to Place Fingers

  • Fingers press down on the fingertip — not the flat pad
  • The angle of your fingertip should be almost vertical to the string
  • Press only as hard as needed to stop the string cleanly. Excess pressure causes cramps.
  • Fingers not in use hover very close above the string — never fly up high. This is important for speed later.
  • Left thumb stays at the side of the neck, not gripping, never pressing hard
  • Curved fingers. Always. If a finger is flat, it's wrong.

02.2

Finger Tapes — The Self-Teaching Cheat Code

Use for the first 3–4 months only

▼

What Are Finger Tapes?

Small pieces of tape placed on the fingerboard to show where each finger goes. A lifesaver for self-taught beginners who can't rely on a teacher correcting their intonation.

How to Place Tapes for D Major / A Major

  • Use thin tape (masking tape, colored tape, or violin finger tape — available online)
  • 1st finger tape: ~3cm from the nut (where neck meets body)
  • 2nd finger tape (high 2nd): ~1cm after 1st tape
  • 3rd finger tape: ~1cm after 2nd tape
  • Verify using your tuner: 1st finger on A string should give B, 3rd should give D, etc.
  • These are guides only — train yourself to move slightly off the tape as your ears develop
  • Remove tapes completely by Month 4–5. Playing with tapes forever prevents proper intonation development.
02.3

Scales — The Foundation of Everything

Weeks 7–16 daily practice — most important exercise

▼

Why Scales?

Scales train finger muscle memory, bow coordination, intonation, and tone simultaneously. For Indian music, scales are the equivalent of practicing sargam (Sa Re Ga Ma...). Every raga is a scale with specific rules. Time spent on scales is never wasted.

Scale Practice Order (Weeks 7–16)

  • Week 7–8: D major scale, 1 octave, bowing one note per bow stroke (détaché). Very slowly.
  • Week 9–10: D major scale, 2 octaves (G string to E string)
  • Week 10–12: A major scale, 1–2 octaves
  • Week 12–13: G major scale
  • Week 13–14: E major scale
  • Throughout: Use tuner to check each note. If it's sharp or flat, adjust finger position.
  • Slow is correct. A clean slow scale is worth 10 fast sloppy ones.

Scale Practice Technique

  • Use a metronome app (free on phone). Start at 40 BPM, one note per beat.
  • Listen while you play — is each note as clean as the open string tone you worked on in Phase 1?
  • Keep the bow in the sounding point (2–3cm from bridge) while changing fingers
  • Look at your bow hand in a mirror, not your left hand — you should be able to place fingers by feel
  • Lift and place fingers clearly — don't slide. Except for grace notes / gamakas, later.

For Indian Music: Sargam on Violin

  • Indian music uses Sa Re Ga Ma Pa Dha Ni Sa — equivalent to Do Re Mi Fa Sol La Ti Do
  • On violin, the common tonic (Sa) positions are: D (open D string), G (open G string), or A (open A string)
  • Beginners: Use D as Sa. Your D major scale = Sa Re Ga Ma Pa Dha Ni Sa
  • Practice the sargam in D: D E F# G A B C# D — one note per bow
  • Sing the sargam names as you play: "Sa Re Ga Ma Pa Dha Ni Sa" — this builds the ear-hand connection critical for playing by ear
02.4

First Songs — Simple Melodies

Start from Week 10 only after scales sound clean

▼
Do not start songs before Week 10. I know it's tempting. But without solid bow technique and finger placement from Phases 1–2.3, you'll learn the song poorly and be stuck unlearning bad habits for months.

Recommended First Songs (Western)

  • Twinkle Twinkle Little Star — Only uses D and A strings, first 3 fingers. Perfect starter.
  • Mary Had a Little Lamb — Easy string crossings
  • Ode to Joy (Beethoven) — Beautiful, simple, only 4 fingers
  • Long Long Ago — Very slow melody, good for bow control

Recommended First Songs (Indian)

  • Sa Re Ga Ma from Pakeezah / any simple sargam song
  • Vaishnav Jan To (Gandhi's favourite bhajan) — simple, stepwise melody
  • Raghupati Raghav — Simple Indian melody with good range
  • Jana Gana Mana (National Anthem) — 1–2 octave range, clean melody
  • Aye Mere Watan Ke Logo — Slow, emotional, great for tone practice

How to Learn a Song (Right Method)

  • Never learn the whole song at once. Learn it in 2–4 bar segments.
  • Identify the notes first (by ear or from a sheet/notation)
  • Practice each segment slowly with a metronome until it's clean
  • Then connect two segments. Then three. Build like blocks.
  • If you make an error 3 times in a row at a certain spot — slow down the metronome and fix that spot specifically
  • Recording rule: Record yourself playing the song every week. Compare to the previous week. This is your feedback loop without a teacher.

Months 4–8

Phase 3: Tone Control & Technique

Your scales are clean, your first songs are working. Now you develop control — dynamics, bow strokes, intonation refinement, and the beginnings of vibrato.

Phase 3 Goal: Be able to play a simple 2-page piece smoothly, with dynamics (loud and soft). Have a stable, warm tone in all bow speeds. Begin attempting vibrato (even if it's rough). Remove finger tapes completely by end of Phase 3.
03.1

Bow Strokes — The Language of Expression

Month 4–5

▼

Détaché (Detached)

One note per bow stroke. Your default stroke. Clean, clear, separated notes. This is what you've been doing all along in scale practice. Now make it intentional and controlled. Practice: scales with perfectly even bow distribution, same contact point every stroke.

Legato (Slurred)

Multiple notes on one bow stroke. The bow continues moving in one direction while fingers change. This creates a smooth, connected sound — essential for Indian music gamakas and lyrical passages. Practice: play 2 notes per bow stroke on scales, then 4 per stroke.

Staccato (Short, Stopped)

Short, crisp notes with the bow staying on the string between notes (bow doesn't lift). Created by stopping the bow suddenly. Sounds like "tuk tuk tuk." Useful for rhythmic passages. Practice: play scale with short, stopped strokes. Feel the bow grip the string and stop abruptly.

Dynamics — Loud and Soft

  • Louder (forte): More bow weight (press arm down slightly) + stay closer to bridge
  • Softer (piano): Less weight + move contact point slightly toward fingerboard + slower bow
  • Crescendo: Gradually increase weight and bow speed across several notes
  • Diminuendo: Gradually reduce weight and speed
  • Practice: play a 4-note phrase from very soft to very loud and back. This is foundational for expressiveness.
03.2

Intonation — Playing in Tune

Ongoing battle — Month 4 onwards

▼
Intonation is never "done." Professional violinists still consciously monitor their intonation. As a self-taught student, this is your biggest challenge. Use the methods below as your teacher.

The Open String Check

Play a finger note (e.g., 3rd finger on D string = G). Then play the open G string. They should be the same pitch — they should ring together without wavering. If they clash, adjust your 3rd finger until they agree. This is your most accurate intonation check on violin.

Tuner-Assisted Intonation Practice

  • Play scales very slowly with your phone tuner open next to you
  • Watch the needle: is it green (in tune) or red (sharp/flat)?
  • When a note is consistently flat → finger needs to move slightly toward the bridge (up)
  • When consistently sharp → finger moves slightly toward nut (down)
  • Do not watch the tuner while playing songs — only during slow scale practice

Singing While Playing

Sing the note you are playing while you play it. If your voice and violin match, you're in tune. This is the most powerful intonation tool. It directly trains your ear and forces your fingers to adjust to match what you hear internally. Start this habit in Phase 2, intensify in Phase 3.

Double Stops for Intonation

Play two strings simultaneously (e.g., open D + 3rd finger on A string = D and D an octave apart). Listen for beats/wavering — when in tune, the two notes "lock" and seem to merge into one sound. This is more accurate feedback than a tuner for learning to trust your ear.

03.3

Vibrato — The Soul of Violin Sound

Begin Month 5–6 (do not rush this)

▼
Important: Do not attempt vibrato before your intonation is stable and your left hand is relaxed. A tense hand cannot produce good vibrato. Vibrato learned incorrectly creates bad habits that are very hard to undo.

What is Vibrato?

Vibrato is a slight, rhythmic oscillation of pitch — the finger rocks back and forth slightly on the string, creating a warm, expressive wavering in the sound. You hear it in all classical violin, film music, and Indian (Carnatic/Hindustani) violin — especially in sustained notes.

Types of Vibrato

  • Arm vibrato — Motion from the forearm/elbow. Wide, slow vibrato. Suits emotional, slow phrases.
  • Wrist vibrato — Motion from the wrist. More agile. Most common in Western classical.
  • Finger vibrato — Motion from the finger knuckle. Very subtle. Advanced.
  • For Indian music: arm and wrist vibrato are both used. Carnatic violin style uses very expressive, wide vibrato.

Learning Vibrato Step-by-Step

  • Step 1 (No violin): Place 2nd finger on a flat surface. Rock it back and forth from the knuckle. Watch the motion. Feel it.
  • Step 2: On violin neck, place 2nd finger, no bow. Rock it back and forth on the string — toward the scroll and back. You should hear the pitch wobble slightly.
  • Step 3: Add a very slow bow on the A string, 2nd finger down. Attempt slow, wide rocking. Focus on motion, not sound quality.
  • Step 4: Gradually speed up the rocking motion over weeks until it becomes a smooth oscillation
  • Step 5: Transfer to all four fingers on all strings
  • Practice vibrato for 10 minutes every day separately from your song practice
03.4

Ornaments & Gamakas (Indian Focus)

Month 6–7 — Essential for Indian Sound

▼

Why Gamakas Are Critical

Indian music's soul is in its ornaments — the slides, deflections, shakes, and oscillations that give each raga its unique flavor. Without gamakas, Indian music on violin sounds like a piano — technically correct but lifeless. These are learned by ear, not from books.

Basic Ornaments to Learn

  • Meend (Slide/Glide): Sliding from one note to another without lifting the finger. On violin: achieved by sliding the finger along the string while bowing. Fundamental to Hindustani music.
  • Kan Swar (Grace Note): A quick touch on a neighboring note before landing on the main note. Like a flick of the finger.
  • Andolan (Oscillation): Slow, deliberate oscillation between a note and a half-step below. Like a wide, controlled vibrato on a specific interval. Common in ragas like Bhairavi.
  • Gamaka (General): Any ornament in Carnatic music. Begin by imitating — listen to a passage, then try to reproduce the ornament on violin. Don't over-theorize — imitation is the primary method.

How to Practice Ornaments

  • Pick one ornament type per week
  • Listen to a song that uses it heavily (e.g., any L. Subramaniam recording for Carnatic gamakas)
  • Pick one note in the song, isolate the gamaka, loop it 20 times
  • Reproduce it on your violin until it matches what you hear
  • This builds the most important muscle: the ear-hand imitation loop

Months 7–12

Phase 4: Playing Songs by Ear

This is your target destination. Your technique is now functional. The focus shifts from mechanics to musicianship — listening, internalizing, and reproducing songs from memory and ear alone.

Phase 4 Goal: Hear an Indian or English song. Hum it. Find the first note on the violin. Work out the rest of the notes by ear. Play it recognizably within 30–60 minutes of hearing it for the first time.
04.1

The Ear-to-Violin Process

The exact method for playing by ear

▼
1

Listen deeply to the song first

Listen to the full song 3–5 times without touching the violin. Hum or sing along. Identify the melody line (not the beats or harmony — just the melody). Can you sing/hum the melody note-by-note? If yes, proceed.

2

Find the first note

Sing the first note of the song. Now find that pitch on your violin by trial and error. Try different strings and finger positions until the violin note matches what you're singing. This is the most important skill — matching a sung pitch to a string position.

3

Work note-by-note through the first phrase

Sing the second note. Find it on violin. Repeat for every note in the first 2–4 bars. Don't jump ahead. Go phrase by phrase. The first time you do this it will take 30+ minutes for 4 bars. Over time it gets much faster.

4

Note the pattern (write it down or memorize)

Note which finger on which string gives each note. After a few attempts, patterns become obvious — this song uses mostly D and A strings, this phrase uses fingers 1-2-3 on A string, etc.

5

Play the phrase, refine, extend

Play the found phrase cleanly. Then repeat step 2–4 for the next phrase. Gradually build the whole song. Add ornaments and expression once the notes are correct.

6

Practice until it's in your fingers

Play the full song from start to finish slowly. Then faster. Revisit tricky spots. Record yourself and compare to the original. Fix what doesn't match. Repeat over 3–7 sessions until you can play it without thinking.

04.2

Finding the Tonic (Sa / Root Note)

The most important skill for Indian music

▼

What is the Tonic?

Every song and raga revolves around a home note (Sa in Indian music, tonic or root in Western). When you hear a song, identify the note it "comes home to" — the note that feels like rest, completion, resolution. That is the tonic.

Finding the Tonic on Violin

  • Hum the tonic note
  • Try bowing open strings (G, D, A, E) — does any match your hummed note?
  • If none matches, try 1st finger on each open string
  • Continue through each position until you find the match
  • Once found: all other notes of the song are now findable relative to this tonic

Common Song Keys on Violin

  • Sa = D: Open D string. Very natural on violin. Most Indian songs work here.
  • Sa = A: Open A string. Also natural.
  • Sa = G: Open G string. Lower register.
  • Sa = E: Open E string. High and bright — some film songs.
  • If Sa is on a fingered note, that's fine too — just less convenient.

Phase 5: Indian Music Deep Dive

Violin is central to both Carnatic (South Indian) and Hindustani (North Indian) classical music. This phase deepens your understanding of how violin is used in these traditions.

05.1

Violin in Indian Music — Key Differences

How Indian violin playing differs from Western

▼

Sitting Position (Carnatic Style)

Carnatic violin is typically played sitting cross-legged or in a chair with the scroll resting on the right ankle/foot. The violin is pointed away at a downward angle. This is quite different from Western standing/chin-rest style. For a self-taught learner, continue with the shoulder rest and Western hold — it is easier to learn alone, and fully compatible with playing Indian music. The Carnatic position requires more guidance to learn safely.

Continuous Sound & Gamakas

In Indian classical music, the bow rarely stops. The sound is continuous, fluid, and ornamented. Gamakas (ornaments) are not decorations — they ARE the music. The microtonal inflections between notes define which raga you're playing. You cannot play a raga "correctly" without the right gamakas.

Understanding Ragas at a Basic Level

  • A raga is a melodic framework — a specific set of notes (like a scale), combined with rules on how to ascend (arohana) and descend (avarohana), and specific gamakas for certain notes.
  • Each raga has a unique emotional character (rasa): Bhairavi = sorrow, Yaman = romance, Bhairav = early morning, etc.
  • Beginners: learn Raag Yaman first — it uses all sharp notes except Pa, has a very recognizable sound, and is easy to improvise in.
  • Then: Raag Bhairav, Raag Bhairavi, Raag Bhimpalasi
  • Carnatic entry: Mayamalavagowla (the basic scale taught to all beginners), then Kalyani, Mohanam

Tanpura / Drone Practice

In Indian music, you always practice against a drone — the tanpura provides a constant Sa (tonic) drone. This trains your ear to hear where you are relative to Sa at all times. Download the app iTablaPro or Tanpura Droid. Set Sa to D. Practice all scales and gamakas against this drone. This is absolutely essential for Indian music intonation.

Parallel Track

Ear Training — Learn to Listen

Playing by ear is a skill, not a gift. It is trained. This section runs parallel to all five phases — start from Week 1 and never stop.

Daily Ear Training (5–10 min)

  • Interval singing: Play a note on violin, sing it, then sing the next note a specific interval away (half step, whole step, major 3rd, etc.), then find that note on violin
  • Melodic dictation: Listen to 4–8 bars of a song on loop, then try to reproduce it on violin note by note
  • Sargam singing: Sing Sa Re Ga Ma Pa Dha Ni Sa while playing. Do it backwards. Jump intervals.
  • Active listening: When listening to any music, hum the melody actively. Identify the swar (notes). Guess what instruments are used. Track the melody line.

The Three Listening Levels

  • Passive: Music plays in background. Brain doesn't analyze. No benefit for ear training.
  • Active: Music plays, you focus fully. Track melody, identify patterns, anticipate next note. 20 min/day of this accelerates ear development dramatically.
  • Critical: Compare two versions of the same song. Identify what changed in the melody. Notice intonation differences. This is advanced ear training — start from Phase 3.

Most important rule: Sing everything before playing it. If you can't sing a phrase, you can't reliably play it by ear.

Your Biggest Asset: Growing up hearing Indian film music, bhajans, and classical music has already trained your ear for complex melodic ornamentation, microtones, and long melodic phrases. Most Western students cannot hear these nuances. You have a 5-year head start on ear training compared to someone who grew up only hearing Western pop music. Trust your ear.

Rules

Do's & Don'ts — The Complete List

Read this every month. Print it out if you need to. These are the rules self-taught violinists most often violate.

✓ Always Do These

  • Tune before every single practice session
  • Warm up before playing — physical warmup (fingers, wrists, shoulders)
  • Practice slowly and cleanly before speeding up
  • Use a metronome for all scale and technical work
  • Record yourself at least once a week
  • Practice with a drone when working on Indian music
  • Take breaks every 20–25 minutes
  • Sing every melody before playing it
  • Check your posture in a mirror at least weekly
  • Loosen bow after every session
  • Wipe rosin off the violin and strings after every session
  • Work on the hardest parts of a song first, not last
  • Listen to professional violinists playing the music you want to play
  • Be patient — violin is a long journey and progress is not always linear
  • Rest the left hand thumb gently — always
  • Practice without looking at left hand — develop feel
  • Keep fingers curved and close to the string when not in use
  • Store violin in case after every session

✗ Never Do These

  • Never squeeze the neck with thumb and index finger
  • Never tense your shoulders — check and consciously drop them
  • Never press too hard with the bow — it will not make more sound, only distortion
  • Never learn a song at full speed before learning it slowly
  • Never skip tuning — even if you're "just practicing for a minute"
  • Never practice through hand or wrist pain
  • Never allow the violin to droop — scroll should be up
  • Never touch bow hair with fingers
  • Never leave bow at full tension when not playing
  • Never place violin on its back on a hard surface
  • Never practice right before sleeping if it causes finger/arm soreness
  • Never jump to vibrato before intonation is stable
  • Never practice scales so fast that notes are uneven — slow down
  • Never skip the warmup in a hurry to get to song practice
  • Never buy cheap rosin or use another instrument's rosin — viola/cello rosin is too heavy for violin
  • Never use sandpaper or abrasive cleaners near the violin body
  • Never force a peg if it's stuck — use peg paste or take to a shop
  • Never learn a song with wrong notes and drill them — fix immediately

Self-Check

Common Mistakes — Diagnose Yourself

This is your self-diagnosis guide. When something sounds wrong, find the symptom below and fix the root cause.

Sound Problems

Scratchy / Harsh Sound

  • Bow too close to the bridge → move contact point slightly toward fingerboard
  • Too much bow pressure → lighten the arm weight on the bow
  • Too much rosin on bow hair → wipe bow hair gently with a dry cloth
  • Bow speed too slow with too much pressure → increase bow speed

Airy / Weak / No Sound

  • Not enough rosin → apply 5–8 strokes of rosin
  • Bow too far from bridge (over fingerboard) → move toward bridge
  • Bow speed too fast with too little pressure → slow bow, add slight weight
  • Bow hair too loose → tighten slightly (1cm gap)

Squeaking / Sudden High Pitch

  • Bow sliding onto wrong string → control arm height more carefully
  • Bow slipping off contact point → maintain consistent 2–3cm from bridge
  • Bow angle not perpendicular to string → bow must be straight, parallel to bridge

Wolf Note (Sudden Loud Resonance)

Some notes on violin "wolf" — an uncontrolled resonance. Often around E on D string or specific notes depending on the instrument. Fix: slightly increase bow pressure and change bow speed when passing through the wolf note. This is an instrument property, not your fault.

Posture Problems

Neck / Shoulder Pain

  • Using shoulder rest? If not, this is likely cause — install it
  • Hunching to look at fingers → look forward, use mirror instead
  • Violin drooping down → raise scroll to eye level
  • Clenching jaw → relax jaw on chin rest, let it rest naturally
  • Take a break immediately when neck pain occurs

Left Hand/Wrist Pain

  • Squeezing neck with thumb → lighten thumb contact dramatically
  • Wrist bent inward → straighten wrist, bring elbow under violin more
  • Playing too long without break → rest every 20 minutes
  • Fingers pressing too hard → pressing lightly is enough for clear note

Right Arm Fatigue / Shaking

  • Gripping bow too tightly → hold like a small bird — flexible, not gripped
  • Locking elbow → keep elbow joint loose and flexible throughout bow stroke
  • Raising shoulder while bowing → conscious shoulder drop exercise

Intonation Problems

Notes Sound Wrong / Off-Pitch

  • First: is the violin tuned? Check with app.
  • Finger not placed precisely → use tuner to verify each note
  • Thumb causing string deviation → verify thumb is not pressing laterally
  • Not listening while playing → slow down and hear every note
  • Use open string checks: play 3rd finger on D string = should match open G

Intonation Good Slowly But Bad Fast

Speed is the enemy of intonation while learning. Slow practice at perfect intonation "grooves in" the correct finger positions into muscle memory. Playing fast before slow practice ingrains wrong positions. The fix: there is no shortcut — slow the metronome down until the intonation is perfect, then gradually speed up over weeks.
